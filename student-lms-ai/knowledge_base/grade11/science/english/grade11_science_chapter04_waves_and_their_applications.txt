Grade 11 Science – Chapter 04
Unit: Physics
Topic: Waves and Their Applications

Waves:
A wave is a disturbance that propagates through a medium or space. When a disturbance is created, energy is transferred from one point to another without the transfer of matter. Examples include water waves formed by dropping an object on water and waves formed on a rope when it is shaken.

Energy Transfer by Waves:
Waves carry energy from one place to another. During wave motion, particles of the medium oscillate about their mean positions but do not travel with the wave. Energy transfer occurs without transporting the substance of the medium.

Wave Motion:
Wave motion occurs when particles of a medium oscillate and transmit energy through the medium. Waves can propagate through solids, liquids and gases. Some waves can also propagate without a material medium.

Mechanical Waves:
Waves that require a material medium for propagation are known as mechanical waves. Examples include water waves, sound waves and waves on strings. The motion of particles of the medium is essential for mechanical wave propagation.

Classification of Mechanical Waves:
Mechanical waves are classified based on the direction of particle motion relative to the direction of wave propagation. The two main types are transverse waves and longitudinal waves.

Transverse Waves:
Transverse waves are waves in which particles of the medium oscillate perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation. Water surface waves and waves on strings are examples of transverse waves.

Characteristics of Transverse Waves:
In transverse waves, crests are points of maximum upward displacement and troughs are points of maximum downward displacement. The wave travels perpendicular to particle motion.

Longitudinal Waves:
Longitudinal waves are waves in which particles of the medium oscillate parallel to the direction of wave propagation. Sound waves in air are examples of longitudinal waves.

Characteristics of Longitudinal Waves:
Longitudinal waves consist of compressions and rarefactions. Compressions are regions of high particle density and pressure, while rarefactions are regions of low particle density and pressure.

Comparison of Transverse and Longitudinal Waves:
In transverse waves, particle motion is perpendicular to wave direction and they commonly travel on surfaces or strings. In longitudinal waves, particle motion is parallel to wave direction and they propagate through solids, liquids and gases.

Physical Quantities Associated with Waves:
Wave motion involves variations with time and distance. Sinusoidal waves are used to study wave properties.

Amplitude:
Amplitude is the maximum displacement of particles from their mean position during wave motion. It indicates the energy carried by the wave.

Wavelength:
Wavelength is the distance between two consecutive particles in the same state of motion. It is equal to the distance between two successive crests or troughs in transverse waves.

Period:
The period is the time taken by a particle to complete one full oscillation. It is also the time taken by a wave to travel one wavelength.

Frequency:
Frequency is the number of oscillations completed by a particle in one second. It is the reciprocal of the period. The unit of frequency is hertz.

Wave Speed:
Wave speed is the distance traveled by the wave per unit time. It is given by the relationship v = fλ, where v is wave speed, f is frequency and λ is wavelength.

Electromagnetic Waves:
Electromagnetic waves do not require a material medium for propagation. They consist of oscillating electric and magnetic fields that are perpendicular to each other and to the direction of wave propagation.

Speed of Electromagnetic Waves:
Electromagnetic waves travel at a speed of approximately 3 × 10⁸ m s⁻¹ in a vacuum. Their speed in material media is lower than in a vacuum.

Characteristics of Electromagnetic Waves:
Electromagnetic waves are transverse waves. They are not affected by external electric or magnetic fields and do not require a medium for propagation.

Electromagnetic Spectrum:
The electromagnetic spectrum is the range of electromagnetic waves arranged according to frequency or wavelength. It includes gamma rays, X-rays, ultraviolet rays, visible light, infrared rays, microwaves and radio waves.

Gamma Rays:
Gamma rays have extremely high frequencies and energies. They can penetrate thick materials and are used in cancer treatment and sterilization of equipment.

X-Rays:
X-rays are used in medical imaging and security scanning. They are produced when high-speed electrons strike metal targets.

Ultraviolet Radiation:
Ultraviolet radiation has frequencies higher than visible light. It is produced by the sun and artificial sources. Limited exposure helps vitamin D production, while excessive exposure causes health hazards.

Infrared Radiation:
Infrared radiation lies below the red region of visible light. It is emitted by hot objects and produces heating effects. It is used in remote controls, thermal imaging and medical treatments.

Microwaves:
Microwaves have lower frequencies than infrared rays. They are used in microwave ovens, radar systems and communication devices. Water and fats absorb microwaves effectively.

Radio Waves:
Radio waves have the longest wavelengths and lowest frequencies in the electromagnetic spectrum. They are used in long-distance communication and broadcasting.

Sound:
Sound is a form of energy that produces the sensation of hearing. Sound is generated by vibrations of objects.

Propagation of Sound Waves:
Sound waves propagate as longitudinal waves through compressions and rarefactions in a medium. Particles vibrate back and forth around their mean positions.

Medium Requirement for Sound:
Sound requires a material medium to propagate. It does not travel through a vacuum. Sound travels fastest in solids, slower in liquids and slowest in gases.

Speed of Sound:
The speed of sound in dry air at room temperature is about 330 m s⁻¹. The speed increases with temperature. Sound travels faster in water and even faster in solids.

Characteristics of Sound:
The three main characteristics of sound are pitch, loudness and quality. These characteristics help distinguish different sounds.

Pitch:
Pitch is the characteristic of sound related to frequency. Higher frequency sounds have higher pitch, while lower frequency sounds have lower pitch.

Loudness:
Loudness depends on the amplitude of the sound wave. Greater amplitude results in louder sound, while smaller amplitude results in softer sound.

Quality of Sound:
Quality of sound depends on the waveform of the sound. Different instruments produce different waveforms even at the same pitch and loudness.

Hearing Range:
The human hearing range is approximately from 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz. Sounds below this range are infrasonic and sounds above this range are ultrasonic.

Ultrasound:
Ultrasound consists of sound waves with frequencies above 20,000 Hz. It is used by animals like bats and dolphins and in medical imaging, sonar and industrial applications.

Applications of Ultrasound:
Ultrasound is used to measure sea depth, detect underwater objects, examine internal organs, monitor fetal development and break kidney stones.

Musical Instruments:
Musical instruments produce sound through periodic vibrations. They are classified as string instruments, percussion instruments and wind instruments.

String Instruments:
String instruments produce sound through vibrations of stretched strings. The frequency depends on string length, tension and mass per unit length.

Percussion Instruments:
Percussion instruments produce sound by vibrating membranes, rods or metal plates. Pitch depends on the area and tension of the vibrating part.

Wind Instruments:
Wind instruments produce sound through vibrations of air columns. Pitch depends on the length of the air column.

Noise and Music:
Sounds with regular vibrations are pleasant and are called music. Sounds with irregular vibrations are unpleasant and are called noise.

Summary of Waves:
Waves transfer energy without transferring matter. Mechanical waves require a medium while electromagnetic waves do not. Understanding waves is essential for explaining natural phenomena and technological applications.
